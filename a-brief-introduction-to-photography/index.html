Let's begin with a quotation by a very famous landscape photographer. It's a quote I like very much and basically says that in order to truly take a photo, you have to master all its aspect, including the technical aspects.

This is however a bit... categorical so let me adapt it a bit.

So saying that photography is cooking is a bit silly, but let me explain a bit

So all of these are cakes. The first two are black forest cake and the last one is a custom-made Totoro-shaped pound cake. They're all very good. What's different between them ? The first was bought in a shop and the two other were home-made

The point I'd like to make here is that it is perfectly acceptable to let someone or something else do the job for you. But the real fun begins when you begin to make things by yourself. And when you know what you're doing, you come to appreciate (or appreciate) the things that are automatically done for you.

In photography term, that means that auto mode is not necessarily evil, it depends on what you want to achieve with photography. For instance, I use auto mode all the time with my cell phone, because most of the time, when I use it, I want to make a memory of something. However, because I have some experience in photography (I baked my own cake), most people say that I make better pictures than them.

The goal of this class is to make you lean to the side of the home-made than to the side of automatic

Aperture, speed and ISO explained with an ice tray

Photo credits: Jesús Gorriti
Ice tray analogy stolen from Lense.fr

Let me draw my secret weapon :)

So picture an ice tray. The ice tray is the sensor of your camera. You have to fill the sensor with something. With your camera, that will be light. With the ice tray, that will be water.

Now, how do you fill your ice tray ?

Just enough water

Ideally you want to put just enough water.

Too much water

If you put too much water, it overflows and you get a messy freezer.

On the photo it means that everything becomes whitish because of the overflowing light.

Not enough water

If you don't put enough water, you get nearly no ice cubes at the end. On the photo it means there's not enough light to be able to actually see something.

Now, there's several way in which you can fill your ice tray. I see two extremes way:

Small flow rate, long exposure time

Large flow rate, short exposure time

In one extreme, you only let a small trickle of water flow and you wait for a long time under the faucet.

In the other extreme, you can get all hell breaks loose and only put your tray under the water for something like less than a second.

In photography, you can do the same thing: either you let the light come to the sensor in a very limited fashion, or you can just flood the sensor with light for a very short amount of time. Or, most of the time, you do something in between.

Aperture ?

The number that correspond to the different opening of the diaphragm are like these, as power of square root of two. This probably means greek to you and for me as well. The way I see it is: the smaller the number is, the bigger is the opening.

So if you have a big opening (so: small number), it's as if you put the water to maximum flow.

Creative impact of the aperture

So just like shutter speed, aperture has a creative impact. As you can see, it's about bluriness.

So the more open the diaphgram is, the smaller depth of field is.

Depth of field is the volume where object appears to be in focus / sharp.

When you open the diaphgram, the depth of field shrink, and conversely, when you close the diaphgram, the depth of field expand.

Be wary of shooting wide open though. When you have a lens that opens a lot (like this 1.4), what often happens for instance in portrait photography is that the tip of the nose is sharp and but everything else is blurry.

Noise and grain

Analog

Digital

Like the other setting, setting a high or low ISO chnage something on the appearance of the photo.

The effect can be seen in terms of grain in analog photogaphy or noise in digital photography.

In analog photography, grain are due to the size of the grain. You can see in those example that the surface is not totally smooth. But at least it's not colored. Some people like this texture and think they add some personality to the picture

Noise in digital photography are due to the imprecision of the sensor when you put a high voltage on it. Remember that at high voltage, the sensor become are caffein junkie and can somehow hallucinate false color.

You can reduce noise with post processing, but most of the time it obliterates the fine details

When you put your camera in A mode or S mode, the camera decide what the other setting will be. The question is how? And, perhaps more importantly: when does it fail and what can we do about it ?

What the camera would like to see

That color is called medium gray, or gray 18%

Engineer over time have remarked that in average, a well exposed photo will emit as much light as a uniform surface that has a reflectance of 18%, in other word this color

The most basic algorithm for a camera would be to measure the average light that comes from the screne and modify the parameters so as to match this medium gray color.

If you're on A mode, you set the aperture and you have only 1 degree of freedom, and that's by modifying the shutter speed.

Same deal with S mode: you set the shutter speed, the camera modifies the aperture.

If you're on Auto, the camera magically sets what's good.

Photo credits: Fred Benenson

Sometime it fails, for instance here. Overall the illumination of the scene is still 18%, but the nearly all the light is brought by the sun. Doing so, we cannot se the details of the city.

If we wanted to get something out of the city, we would have to tell the camera to not take into account the light coming from the sun when it makes its average.

We can of course do that.

Light metering mode

So there's three light metering mode.

Spot: only 2-3% of the photo is taken into account when making the average

Center weighted: it's a bigger zone that's taken into account.

Matrix / Multi-zone: take into account the whole frame, but instead of doing a dumb average it compares the different reading to an internal database.

Matrix gets good results most of the time but when it fails there's no way to tell why it fails. That's why some photographer prefer using the two arguably more reliable mode.

Exposure lock ("AE-Lock")

Framing is an important part of photography. You will learn about it later on. The problem we have here is that often we don't want to have the subjet of the photo in the center of your picture, so you put your subject off-center. But you still want the subjet to be well exposed. What can you do ?

You can first use matrix light metering and hope for the best.

A second solution would be to set your camera to spot or center, center your subjet, measure light, press the AE lock button and then decenter your subjet. You're then free to take your picture.

Autofocus

Having a good exposure is great, but have a subject that is sharp is better. So what does it mean, to be in focus ?

Photo credit: Mark Levoy

This means that every light ray that goes from a point in the objet will end up at an unique point on the sensor.

To achieve that, the camera maker have devised to solution: phase detection autofocus and contrast detection autofocus.

I won't spend much time on it, since nowadays there's mostly comparable in term of performance and speed.

What's really importan though is to know what you can actually do with autofocus.

So most of the time, in order to actuate the autofocus you have to half-press the shutter release. If you have a more expensive camera, you may have a dedicated button for that ("AF-ON").

So, half press the button, the camera put things in focus. But they are some sublteties:


Photo credit: Nikon, Canon

First, the autofocus point. In your viewfinder, your are free to put things in focus wherever you want in what is called autofocus point. You can thus easily compose you picture without having your subject at the center.
